Perfect Competition: Opium of the Elites
* Economists increasingly assumed markets were 'perfectly competitive' - small number of homogenous commodities, with no individual holding or buying a large fraction of any of them - all are forced to vigorously compete to sell their products and to purchase things they need from others
* Few markets in the real world work this way - as pioneers like Joan Robinson realized - housing markets in large cities seem perfectly competitive but this is a far from perfect system - houses differ in location, amenities, views, light and so forth. The failure to reach a deal can mean months of delay while buyers look for other houses that might meet their needs. 
* Buyers and sellers both have significant bargaining power - each part works hard to ascertain what the other would be willing to pay or accept and jockeys for the best price possible - such strategic behaviour often causes trades to fail -> problems are magnified in complex business transactions - in land development schemes, where many contiguous pieces of land must be bought up to build a factory or a mall, the existing home owners have the upper hand in bargaining because the stakes are so high for the developer - many homeowners will hold out for a large payment, delaying or even stopping the project.
	* Factories, IP, companies, paintings - all are highly idiosyncratic, one of a kind assets (art and anarchism?? art governed by popular taste is usually mediocre)
	* The same holds true for labor markets since all workers have different talents and dispositions and live in different places - even markets for relatively homogenous commodities such as Internet services or airplane flights -> a few dominant firms prevail - even when there appear to be many firms, they frequently share owners or collude
	* From top to bottom, market power - permeates the economy
