1. Preliminaries
1.1. A priori Knowledge and Justification: The Shared View
a priori stands in contrast to the a posteriori or empiricla - knowledge is empirical just in case it is derived from experience 
Experience uncontroversially includes sense perception. It also includes introspection and the testimony of others. A priori knowledge, if there is any, would be independent of sense perception, introspection - justification is necessary for knowledge if one interprets justificiation broadly - so a belief might be justified in virtue of being produced by a reliable cognitive mechanism, or by an exercise of intellectual virtue, or having evidence from good reasons

For one's knowledge to be independent of experience, one' justification would also have to be independent of experience  - It is unlikely S could be justified in believing any proposition P in a way that is completely independent of experience. S must grasp or understand P before S could be justified in believing it - propositions involve properties and in order to understand a proposition, S must have or grasp concepts corresponding to these properties. Without experience S could not believe or even understand most propositions.

In what virtue could S be justified in believing something apart from experience - the standard answer appeals to the special nature of propositions that can be a priori justified - supposed to be self evident
