3.3.4. Technological risks

* This raises not only ethical but other philosophical issues - epistemological and decision-theoretical issues 
* Risk is usually defined as the product of the probability of an undesirable event and the effect of that event - desirable to keep technological risks as small as possible - the larger the risk, the larger the likeliness and impact of an undesirable event - risk reduction is an important goal in technological development- however it is not always feasible or desirable 
* Safer products might be more difficult to use, expensive or less sustainable
* Process of dealing with risks is often divided into three stages - risk assessment, evaluation, risk management - the second is most ethically relevant - risk assessment already involves value judgments- about which risks should be assessed. The degree of evidence needed to establish a risk? In establishing a risk on the basis of a body of empirical data might make two mistakes
	* 1. one might establish a risk when there was none (type 1 error)
	* 2. one might mistakenly conclude there is no risk when there is (type II error)
* Judging the acceptability of risks - comparing to other risks/standards?? ONe might commit a naturalistic fallacy - it might be unavoidable but not acceptable. It is dubious to judge the acceptability of risk in one technology by risk in another technology. 
* Cost benefit analysis??
* Basin risk on the consent of people who suffer the risks after they have been informed about these risks (informed consent). A problem of this approach is that technological risks usually affect a large number of people at once - informed consent may lead to a society of stalemates.
	* alternatives to risk assessment? emotions in judging the acceptability of risk?? Exposure of a person to risk is only acceptable if the exposure is part of an equitable social system of risk taking that works to her advantage -> introducing moral considerations in risk that are traditionally not addressed or only marginally addressed? For example, individuals profit from risky activity and the consideration whether the distribution of risks and benefits is fair.
	* Acceptability of risk imposition - under what conditions is it acceptable for agent A to impose a risk on some other agent B - the criteria for acceptable risk imposition are in part similar to the ones discussed above - a risk imposition may be more acceptable if B gave an informed consent or if the risky activity that generates the risk is beneficial for agent B
* RIsk imposition is problematic if it leads to domination like effects (look at [[Pettit 2012's definition]] ) - freedom from domination does not just require people to have different options to choose from but also free from the potential arbitrarily interference in the availability of these options by others
* Non domination requires that others do not have the power to arbitrary interfere with one's options (whether the power is exercised or not). Risk imposition may lead to domination or at least dominating like effects if agent A the risk imposer by imposing a risk on agent B the risk bearer can arbitrary affect the range of safe options available to it
* But we might lack the knowledge to reliably assess the risks of a new technology before it has come into use - we often do not know the probability that something might go wrong, sometimes we do not know, or not fully, what might go wrong and what possible negative consequences may be
	* -> thinking of the introduction of new technology as a social experiment and thinking about the conditions under which such experimetns are acceptable??
	* Some have stated that a focus on risks has led to a reduction of tech impacts considered - only safety and health are considered as risks, but social/psychological impacts are neglected.
