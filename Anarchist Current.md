Anarchy to Anarchism

* Anarchism is constantly changing - the principles aren't chaos and terrorism but a rejection of hierarchy, authority and exploitation, an alternative vision of society without domination based on freedom and equality - anarchists reject the state and its institutions, advocating societies based on free association, without anyone having the power to dominate or exploit another
* Long before anyone consciously articulated anarchist ideas, people had lived in societies without a state for thousands of years - so called primitive and prehistoric peoples lacked any formal institutions of government and hierarchical social structures based on relationships of command and obedience. Around 6000 years ago the first hierarchical societies began to emerge in which a minority of their members assumed positions of prestige and authority from which they came to exercise power over others 
* It took thousands of years for this process of state formation finally to encompass the entire globe, with some people continuing to live in stateless societies into the 20th century - members of stateless societies lived in roughly egalitarian communities without rank or status. For the most part stateless societies had sustainable subsistence economies based on relationships of equality, reciprocity, and mutual aid (find the evidence for this)

Godwin's Critique of Coercion
* Developed an anarchist critique not only of revolutionary violence but of coercion - institutionalized coercion of the law with its penal systems, or the individual coercion of a parent toward a child - wrote and revised his great philosophical work, An Enquiry Concerning Political Justice, during the French Revolution - published around the time Napoleon was coming into Power
* Coercion and its positive correlate, inducements offered by those with wealth and power, distort political debate and moral discussion by causing people to evaluate a policy or course of conduct in terms of the punishments or rewards attached to them, rather than on their intrinsic merits - coercion and inducements also have a debilitating effect on both persons in power and the people who obey or accept them
	* those who are masters/in power are excused from cultivating their rational faculties - those forced to obey become resentful and fearful - instead of being encouraged to think for themselves, they learn how to avoid detection and seek power for themselves so they can effect their own purposes
* The consequences of coercion and inducement are not surmounted by parliamentary debates or 'deliberative democracy' (Dryzek 2000) - laws and policies of the government are not a result of direct debate among the people but the result of debates of elected representatives who represent particular interests - decisions are made by majority vote of the representatives who vote along party lines -> and the minority is obliged to assist the majority in carrying out the decision into execution since all representatives are required to uphold the law 
* ==During revolutionary upheaval, reasoned and impartial debate can scarcely be pursued - revolutions invariably provoke counter-revolution -> "riveting on us anew the chains of despotism" - "revolutions and violence have too often been coeval with important changes of the social system" -we must take "proper advantage of circumstances as they arise, and not...withdraw ourselves because everything is not conducted according to our ideas of propriety"
* ==The validity of Godwin's warning, based on his observations of the French Revolution may result in a new tyranny because it is the strongest and not the most just who typically triumph, has been borne out by the experience of anarchists in subsequent revolutions
* When anarchist movements began to emerge in 19th century Europe, Godwin's work was relatively unknown. It was largely through the work of the anarchist historian, Max Nettlau, that the ideas of de la Boetie and Godwin were introduced to European anarchists, well after anarchism had emerged as an identifiable current of thought.

Charles Fourier and the Liberation of Desire
* Lived through the French Revolution and witnessed the hoarding and profiteering that occurred during the REvolution and sought to develop a libertarian alternative by which everyone would not only be guarnateed their means of subsistence but would be able to engage in productive work which they themselves found fulfilling
* In order to survive in the emerging capitalist economy, workers were compelled to take whatever work they could find, regardless of their personal talents, aptitudes and preferences - had to work long hours under deplorable conditions, only to see employers reap the fruits of their labors while they continued to live in poverty - the new economy was "nothing but...a league of the minority which possesses, against the majority which does not possess the necessaries of life"
* Didn't advocate revolution - hoped to attract financial benefactors to fund the creation of communes or phalanxes where each person would rotate through a variety of jobs each day, free to choose each task, doing what they found to be enjoyable, giving expression to their talents and passions - guaranteed a minimum of material support and renumerated by dividends from the phalanx's operations - later anarchists found Fourier's more detailed plans re the organization of society too constrictive and the idea that wealthy benefactors would bankroll the abolition of their own privileged status naive

Proudhon: The Self Proclaimed Anarchist
* ==1840 - declared himself an anarchist in the groundbreaking book What is Property? An Inquiry into the Principle of Right and of Government
* Proposed that "property is theft " - he believed that workers should be entitled to the full value of their labour, not the mere pittance capitalists dole out to them while keeping the lion's share for themselves
* That capitalists were parasites exploiting the workers by depriving them of what was rightfully theirs was a common theme in 19th century socialist and anarchist propaganda - 1883 Pittsburgh Proclamation of the International Working People's Association (the 'Black International') - the then anarchist collectivist Johann Most put it this way: 'the propertied (capitalists) buy the working force body and soul of the propertyless, for the mere cost of existence (wages) and take for themselves, i.e steal, the amount of new values (products) which exceeds the price'
* It is government, through its laws and coercive mechanisms, that protects the property of the capitalists, condemning the workers to lives of servitude and misery - the only just form of society is one in which workers are free to associate, to combine their labour, and to exchange what they produce for products and services of equivalent value. In a society based on equivalent exchange there would no longer be any need for government because those things which make government necessary such as "pauperism, luxury, oppression, vice, crime and hunger" would "disappear from our midst." Proudhon described this form of socialism as "mutualism"

Proudhon: Machinery and Worker Self-Management

* Kropotkin continues to influence and inspire green anarchists - Graham Purchase - anarchist form of bioregionalism and Peter Marhsall, with his liberation ecology
* Another aspect of Kropotkin's conception of anarchist communism that had far reaching implications - vision of a free society which "Seeks the most complete development of individuality combined with the highest development of voluntary association in all its aspects"
* Some Italian anarchist communists - Luigi Galleani - argued for an even more fluid concept of voluntary association, opposing any attempts to create permanent orgnaizations, whether an anarchist federation or a revolutionary trade union - any formal organization requies its memers to "submit for the sake of discipline", unity to "provisions, decisions, measures...even though they may be contrary to their opinion and their interest"
	* As Davide Turcato points out - the debate between anti-organizationalists and organizationalists was a debate of great sophistication - developing many ideas to become common currency in the sociological literature
		* anarchists were the first to insist upon the hierarchical and oligarchi consequences of party organization
	* Most anarchist communists including Kropotkin and Malatesta believed that non hierarchical organization is possible and desirable - one must guard against oligarchic and bureaucratic tendencies

Means and Ends:
* Some thought ideals sprung rom deeds, not the other way round
* Kropotkin argued exemplary actions "which compel general attention, the new idea seeps into people's minds and wins converts. One such act may, in a few days, make more propaganda than thousands of pamphlets"
* At the 1881 International Anarchist COngress in London - the delegates declared themselves in favour of "illegality" as "the only way leading to revolution"
* After years of state persecution, a small minority of self-proclaimed anarchists adopted terrorist tactis in the 1890s - Anarchist groups had been suppressed in Spain, Germany, and Italy in the 1870s, particularly after some failed assassination attempts on the Kaiser in Germany and the Kings of Italy and SPain in the late 1870s, even before Russian revolutionaries assassinated Czar Alexander II in 1881. Although none of the would be assassins were anarchists, the authorities and capitalist press blamed the anarchists and their doctrine of propaganda by deed for these events - with the Times of London describing anarchism in 1879 as having "Revolution for its starting point, murder for its means, and anarchy for its ideals"
* Anarchists in France who survived the Paris Commune were imprisoned, transported to penal colonies, or exiled. During the 1870s and 1880s, anarchists were prosecuted for belonging to the First International. Some anarchists in 1883 were imprisoned on the basis of alleged membership.
* Haymarket Martyrs - convicted and condemned to death on trumped up charges that they were responsible for throwing a bomb at a demonstration in the Chicago Haymarket area in 1886
* In the 1890s, the French state brought in draconian laws banning anarchist activities and publications - the state was far more violent
* Malatesta argued "bourgeois society cannot be overthrown" by bombs and knife blows because it is based "on an enormous mass of private interests and prejudices...sustained...by the inertia of the masses and their habits of submission". He called on anarchists to "live among the people and to win them over...by actively taking part in their struggles and sufferings," for the anarchist social revolution can only succeed when the people are "ready to fight and...to take the conduct of their affairs into their own hands"

Anarchism and the Workers' Struggles
* The International Working People's Association (IWPA) drew its inspiration from the anti-authoritarian International and adopted a social revolutionary anarchist program at its founding Congress in Pittsburgh - openly advocating armed insurrection and revolutionary expropriation of the capitalists by the workers themselves
* the IWPA sought to create revolutionary trade unions that would press for the immediate demands of the workers, for example the 8 hour day, while preparing for social revolution - similar ideas were being propounded by the Workers' Federation of the SPanish REgion and by anarchists invovled in working class movements in Latin America
* Sensing growing disillusionment among the workers with the electoral tactics of the socialist parties, some anarchists had again become involved in the trade union movement - through participation in trade unions, anarchists "taught the masses the true meaning of anarchism, a doctrine" which can readily "manage without the individual dynamiter (Fernand Pelloutier)"
* Pelloutier argued that revolutionary trade union organizations are based on voluntary membership and therefore operate largely on the basis of free agreement - union officials can be revoked and play a coordinating rather than directorial role -> through their own autonomous organizations workers will come "to understand they should regulate their affairs for themselves" and can prevent the reconstitution of state power after erevolution by taking control of "the instruments of production"
* Endorsed anarchist communism as the ultimate goal of the revolutinoary syndicalist movement
* However the revolutionary syndicalist organization (the Confederation Generale du Travail) adopted a policy of nonaffiliation with any party or doctrine - focused on the means of revolutionary action, such as direct action and the general strike, instead of arguing over ideology
* Anarcho-syndicalist union federations sought to abolish the state and capitalism by the means of general strikes, factory occupations, expropriation, and insurrection rejecting statism, even the "Dictatorship of the Proletariat" - because dictatorship "will always be the creator of new monopolies and new privileges"
* Anarchists who sought to work within revolutionary working class organizations or popular movements adopted different approaches regarding the proper relationship between anarchist ideals and broader based social movements - some argued anarchists needed their own organizations to coordinate activities, support work within trade unions, and to spread their ideas - the model of dual organization was similar to what Baqkunin had advocated during the First Interational, when he urged his comrades to revolutionary brotherhood, the Alliance of Social Revolutionaries which adhered to Bakunin's anarchist program
* Preventing the development of a trade union bureaucracy and a de factor executive assuming control of the organization 
* Neno Vasco warned of the dangers of self-proclaimed anarchist groups "populated more by rebels than by aanarchists" seizing the initiative and forcing "emanciaiption on the pepole" by claiming "The right to act on its behalf" instead of prompting the people "to look to its own liberation", with "The persons concerned" taking matters "directly in hand"

Libertairan Education
* Fracisco Ferrer - inspired the "Modern School" movement which sought to liberate children from teh authoritarian structures of religious and state controleld schools - creating schools outside of the existing education system in which children would be free to pursue their individual inclinations and interestss= 
* Making children "accusomted to obey, to believe, to think according to the social dogmas which govern us" and education "but a means of domination in the hands of the governing powers"
* In France, Sebastien Faure founded la Ruche Beehive free school- noteworthy for providing boys and girls with equal educational opportunities, sex education, its rejection of any form of punishment or constraint- radical approaches during an era when girls were excluded or segregated, information regarding sex and contraception was censored even for adults, and corporal punishment of studnets was routine
	* Faure rejected any system of punishments and rewards because "it makes no appeal" to the child's reasoning or conscience, producing "a slavish, cowardly, sheepish breed...capable of cruelty and abjection"
* Herbert Read - expanded on the role of modern education in creating a submissive populace - "everything personal, everything which is the expression of individual perceptions and feelings, is either neglected, or subordinated to some conception of normality, of social convention, of correctness" - advocating libertarian education, emphasizing the creative process and "eduation through art" - it "is only in so far as we liberate" children, "shoots not yet stunted or distorted by an environment of hatred and injustice, that we can expect to make any enduring change in society"
* by the 1960s and 1970 people were again experimenting with libertarian education

Women's Liberation
* Louise Michel - discrimination against girls and women was the greatest barrier to "equality of the sexes". What women "want is knowledge and education and liberty"
* Bakunin opposed the legal institution of marriage
* Carmen Lareva- an early anarchist feminist in Argentina who wrote for La Voz de Mujer - one of the first explicitly anarchist feminist papers written by and for women - decried how the anarchist advocacy of "free love" was distroted by opponents of anarchism into the claim anarchists wanted to liberate women only to turn them "into cocubines" - argued it was existing society, with its inequality, sexual hypocrisy, and exploitation, which drove women into prostitution and forced them into marraiges in which the woman "is required to feign love of someone she simply detests" in exchange for food and housing
* Emma Goldman - demanded the independence of woman, right to support herself, live for herself, love whomever she pleases or as many as she pleases
* Real sexual liberation meant that women should have free access to contraception so that they could be sexually active while being free to decide whether and when to have children - both Goldman and the American anarchist Ezra Heywood - were imprisoned by US authorities were trying to make birth control information and devices available to women

Anarchist Morality
* True morality can only exist between equals - inequality gives rise to "deceit, cunning, exploitation, depravity, vice...It is in the name of equality that we are determined to have no more prostituted, exploited, deceived and governed men and women"
* The sense of justice and solidarity "which brings the individual to consider the rights of every other individual as equal to his own" has been successively widened - for Kroptokin, acting morally is not only natural, but a means of self-fulfilment
* What anarchists sought to achieve was a world in which everyone is free to develop his or her talents and abilities to their fullest
	* Impossible as long as workers are required to engage in labour to eke out an existence, women have to work and obey everyone, and children are trained to accept their lot in life and obey their betters

Art and Anarchy:
* Charlotte Wilson - "when each worker is free to do as nature prompts, to throw his whole soul into the labour he has chosen and make it the spontaneous expression of his intensest purpose and desire...labour becomes pleasure, and its produce a work of art*
* For artists in bourgeois society - must sell works to sruvive - a situation which leads those to compromise to make vulgar and mediocre art
* Artists also have to work for the social revolution - the morality of those who condemn sellouts is problematic
* OScar Wilde thought in The Soul of Man Under Socialism that with the abolition of private property, all will be free "to choose the sphere of activity that is really congenial to them, and gives them pleasure"
	* Spoke in favour of anarchist socialism as the basis for true individualism and artistic freedom - authority over the artist is ridiculous whether exercised by a government or by public opinion - public opinion is "the rule of universal mediocrity"
	* In the turn of the century France, much of the artistic avant garde allied themselves with anarchism, including painters such as Camille Pissarro, Paul Signant, Charles Maurin, Maximilien Luce, writers like Paul Adam, Adolphe Rette, Octave Mirbeau, Bernard Lazare
* The anarchist critique of existing society was never limited to denouncing the state, capitalism, and the church - extended to the patriarchal family, sexual exploitation and subjection of women, censorship, conformism, authoritarian education, and hierarchical and coercive forms of organization

Science and Technology: Anarchist Perspectives
* Denounced machinery which, "after having degraded the worker by giving him a master, completes his degeneracy by reducing him from the rank of artisan to that of common labourer"
* Other anarchists have argued once people take control of technology, it can be redesigned to eliminate onerous toil, to make workplaces safer and to increase production for the benefit of all
* Gustav Landaeur took a more critical position, arguing in 1911 "the capitalist system, modern technology, and state centralism go hand in hand...Technology, allied with capitalism, makes [the worker] a cog in the wheels of the machine"
	* consequently, technology developed under capitalism cannot provide the basis for a free society - workers must step out of capitalism mentally and physically and begin creating alternative communities and technologies designed to meet their needs in conditions which they thesmelves find agreeable
	* Paul Goodman suggested some criteria "for the humane selection of technology: utility, efficiency, comprehensiblity, repairability, ease and flexibility of use, amenity and modesty" [[criteria for tech ethics]]
		* These would turn into convivial tools
* Nineteenth century anarchists often extolled the virtues of modern science, particularly in contrast to religious belief, as part of their critique of the role of organized religion supporting the status quo - Proudhon looked forward to "scientific socialism" and Jose Llunas Pujols looked forward to the supplanting of the political State and theology by Administration and Science - echosing Saint Simon's comment that the government of man would be replaced by the "administration of things"
* Bakunin warned on the limitations of science in his essay God and the State - discussed the limitations of scientific theory and research and the dangers of entrusting affairs to sceintists and intellectuals - warned science couldn't go outside the sphere of abstractions and grasp or aprpeciate the existential reality of individual human beings - otherwise people would be fleeced in the name of science
* Kroptokin never suggested scientists should have a privileged role in society, nor that scientific hypotheses should be regarded as akin to human laws that need to be enforced by some authority - he looked forward to "a society in which all the mutual relations of its members are regulated, not by laws, not by authorities, whether self-imposed or elected, but by mutual agreement...and by a sum of social customs and habits- not petrified byh law, routine or superstition, but continually developing and continually readjusted in accordance with the ever gorwing requirements of a free life, stimulated by the progress of science, invention, and the steady growth of higher ideals.
