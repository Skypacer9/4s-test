
Philosophy of Praxis

* Philosophy of praxis as a figure of thought
* The failure of the proletarian revolution has not ended the struggle against reification but it has fragmented that struggle 
* Reified rationality still prevails in society - mediated by tensions between its forms and the content of the life processes it shapes and constrains - no longer plausible to envisage a final end of this condition - reification is an essential aspect of modernity 
* Both the successes and the failures of the socialist revolution teach us that reified rationality as embodied in social institutions will always confront suppressed potentialities motivating transformation from below
	* right now it doesn't take the form of revolutionary struggle
	* Praxis - revolution is unlikely, but one can still establish a framework of meaning within which activity goes on - if changes in that hidden dimension are understood as essentially transformative, then the philosophy of praxis survives the loss of the revolutionary guarantee (is this essential? why was it lost?)
* Philosophy of praxis is based on a metacritique of idealist philosophy - it attempted to resolve contradictions between subject and object, value and fact, freedom and necessity, life and thought - the idealist resolution took the form of a speculative play with concepts. ==Philosophy of praxis demands a real resolution through the practical transformation of the social basis of antimonies. 
	* the social reconstitution of idealist categories, the construction of the relations between reconstituted categories according to the original idealist pattern, the projection of a resolution of the contradictions between them through social change 
* Philosophers of praxis argue that abstract philosophical categories are sublimated versions of concrete social realities - the philosophical subject is a sublimated version of the real subject, social class. The object is not being or the thing in itself but the actual object of social practice. The antimonies reflect fundamental problems of capitalist society - if value and fact stand opposed - this is because the capitalist world is structured by economic laws indifferent to human needs, and because these two domains of being are by nature alien to each other
	* Marx's dictum "The philosophers have only interpreted the world in various ways; the point is, to change it"
* Philosophy of praxis avoids the crude economic reductionism of which Marxism is often accused by reconstructing the relations idealism established between its categories as social relations - in early Marx labour engages with nature not just technically but ontologically, as power revealing nature within the social world. In lukacs, once the philosophical subject is redefined as the proletariat it is shown to constitute social reality through a process of mediation - in both Marx and Lukacs the idealist concepts of synthesis, constitution, mediation are translated as practical relations to nature and society
* Adorno and Marcuse's claims for social practice are less ambitious but even they consider the subject-object and value-fact relations as unities fractured by capitalism (was it really just capitalism?)
* The vaunted objectivity of science and its restricted empiricism is won at the expense of a richer experience of things and their potentialities - the excluded second dimension is reduced to an inner realm of  subjective values 
* Marx argues the antinomies arise from conceptualizing subject and object on the model of cognition - the real subject and object are worker and nature, not knower and known - Marx argues that the subject of need and its objects are essentially related . The constituting process is played out in the practical humanization of nature through labour and sense experience
	* Marx calls this 'objectification' but under capitalism it takes an alienated form - despite the fundamental interdependence of human subject and object, labouring is associated with povery and loss - the subject object relation is lived in suffering and conflict - private property and wage labour are historically contingent causes of this lived version of the antimony
	* Overcoming these institutional obstacles through revolution can bring about a rational harmony of subject and object no amount of philosophical speculation can achieve
* Lukacs' metacritique relates the subject-object concept of idealism to the economic processes analyzed in Marx's mature works - restricting the early metacritique to a limited social arena and abandoning the larger philosophical goals - Lukacs recovers Marx's original intent to creat  a philosophy of praxis
	* Lukacs lives in a more advanced society where vast bureuacracies deploy a scientific-technical production system  - the socialist reconciliation of man and nature now appears in a different light - rationality has split in two - one form corresponding into a strictly instrumental relation to human beings and nature (reification), another dialectical rationality belongs to the transforming practice in which the social and cultural world are revolutionized by the proletariat - no new concept of reason seems to be able to reconcile them nor can the rule of assembled producers eliminate the instrumental relation to reality 
	* Lukacs - immanent critique of German idealism - three demands of reason that emerge from idealism's failed attempt to carry out its program - the principle of practice, history as reality, and the dialectical method - the historical transformation will take place not through a deterministic mechanism or an unbounded creative process but by realizing the social potentialities revealed in the tension between the rationalized social forms and individual life processes - the philosophical subject of idealism is replaced by the human subject of work - Lukacs emphasizes the role of the subject in constituting and overthrowing reified social institutions rather than its relation to nature 
	* In Marx and Lukacs the concept of reason emerges transformed from the metacritique - the reified form is repalced or complemented by a dialectical rationality -> this is the articulation of social expereince, social experience tied to consciousness 
	* Now rationality is associated not only with science and experiment, but also with the practical critique coming from those subordinated to forms of capitalism - their situated knowledge reveals aspects of reality to which reified rationality is blind
		* The situated knowledge is granted cognitive status by philosophy of praxis - it is not merely 'a matter of opinion'  - it arises from a real object, the lifeworld (consciousness)- theory builds on the critical method immanently present in lifeworldly experience, not a pure reason in which philosophy of praxis no longer belives
		* THis contrasts with the scientistic ideology that becomes increasingly influential from Lukacs' time down to the present - according to this ideology, experience is essentially flawed and subjective in contrast with the objective understanding of a purified cogito, freed from all bonds of corporality, emotino and situation - philosophy of praxis dismisses the view from nowhere as a reflex of reification and imposes a finite horizon on the cognitive subject - it rejects idealism's thesis on the absolute autonomy of reason- relativises naturalism by restricting scientific cognition to a limited aspect of the real-
	* Frankfurt school - theory and practice are split apart - Horkheimer, Adorno, Marcuse conclude no proletarian movement promises to realize the demands of reason and reconcile humanity and nature - the Soviet Union resembles Weber's iron cage of bureaucracy far more than Marx's Paris Commune. Instrumental reason has triumphed and socialist revolution now appears as a lost causes, at least for the foreseeable future.  The third moment in the metacritique is beyond reach - first two moments retain their credibility as a critique of idealism and naturalism, and a reconstructive alternative. It is impossible to detach the first two moments entirely from the world. If subject and object have become concrete, active social beings, their antimonies demand resolution in the world.
		* Adorno - dystopian despair in history - thought and experience of individuals in capitalist society have been radically distorted by the commodification of every aspect of social life (was preindustrial really that great for peasants? what images should we have? what do we know about those times/). Abstract concepts replace a deeper understanding of the connections and potentialities of things. Experience is reduced to a mere reflection of the given, without the imaginative force required for self reflection. Instrumental rationality, unrestrained by any concept of potentialities and limits, threatens human survival with the increase in its technological power to exploit and destroy.
		* ==In Marcuse's late work he arrives at a more positive position under the New Left - they practice a cultural politics that while, lackign the power of a proletarian movement, goes beyond mere technical manipulation within the existing system - the movement testifies to the continuing possibility of practical critique, beyond the one-dimensionality of reified experience under capitalism. Radical experience, the 'new sensibility' is informed by an imaginative grasp of the blocked potentialities of a society that artificially maintains competition, poverty, and war long after it has become rich and secure enough to dispense with these vestiges of an obsolete reality principle

		* Marcuse is the first philosopher of praxis to respond positively to the growth of technology - he argues that radical change must involve not only social transformation but also technological transformation 
			* weak points: reliance on a normative grounding in the affirmation of life, which is intuitively appealing but vague
			* the undifferentiated critique of science and technology that leads to the implausible demand for a new science
			* Marcuse revived the philosophy of praxis - he succeeds in opening up new ways of thinking about technology and experience and the theory-practice relationship that would result from transformation

The Dialectic of Reason and Experience 
* According to Marxism, the proletariat is the subject of socialist revolution - Marx and Lukacs share this notion, even Adorno and Horkheimer in their 1956 dialogue can imagine no other revolutionary agent - why this emphasis on the proloetariat - reasons - the spread of technology into the factory transforms work, workers, the control of productions, creating a compact social subject motivated to overthrow the system - the unfolding dynamic of capitalist crisis makes it ever more difficult to sustain the population, and the emergence of a growing and better informed class of workers promises a democratic alternative to capitalist management of the system - the combination of motivation, crisis, and capacity underpins the classical doctrine of socialist revolution
* Unexpectedly, 20th century capitalism in advanced countries adjusted sufficiently to the threat from below to defuse revolutionary motivations - at the same time, the spread of technology and management continues to produce resistant, if non-revolutionary subjects in many areas of social life- tension filled relation to the reified structures that form them - these struggles have not been totalized in a singular confrontation with capital, the fact that no inevitable economic laws promise victory - but history has not ended for that matter
* A new paradigm of social change is needed based not on the French and Russian revolutions but on a dialectical concept of mediation 
* Lukac's claim in the beginning of History and Class Consciousness - the essence of Marxism consists not in factual claims but in dialectical method - this is not only the theoretical method of Capital but the method of practical critique arising in resistance to reified markets, bureuacracies, technologies 
* Modern society is inherently divided into reified subjects and objects by the systems that found it - these systems are held together in dialectically divided totalities by their rational form - they impose those forms as the basis of social order in every sphere - they are held together in aggregates - their heterogenous content is never absorbed completely by the forms nor is it indifferent to the incomplete process of formation imposed on it - the social rationality of these systems is based on specific cognitive achievements, natural and social scientific insights that determine coherent configurations of human and natural resources - these are real achievements for the most part, not arbitrary or merely ideological
