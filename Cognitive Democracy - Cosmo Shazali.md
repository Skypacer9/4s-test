Cognitive democracy:
* Unique benefits as a form of collective problem solving - allowing people with highly diverse perspectives to come together to collectively solve problems

* The most troublesome problems are those without obvious solutions - how do we change legal rules and social norms in order to mitigate the problems of global warming? regulating financial markets to minimize the risk of new crises emerging and limiting harms?
* These problems are pressing and difficult to think about systematically
	* 1. social problems - involving the interaction of large numbers of human beings with different interests, desires, needs and perspectives
	* 2. they are complex problems - in the sense that scholars of complexity understood the term 
		* They are the result of behaviour that is difficult to predict so that consequences of changing behaviour is difficult to map out in advance 
Macro institutinos will be able to tackle these problems if they have two features
	1. a high degree of direct communication between individuals with diverse viewpoints - this kind of intellectual diversity is crucial to identifying good solutions to complex problems
	2. providing relative equality among affected actors in decision making processes - preventing socially or politically powerful groups from blocking socially beneficial changes to the detriment of their own particular interests
Contentions based on two set of arguments
	difficult problems present many peaks, solutions that are better than the poitns close to them
	There is no guarantee that any peak is globally optimal rather than locally optimal
		solving a complex problem involves a search across this landscape for the best visible solutions
			Individual agents have limited cognitive abilities and limited knowledge of the landscape, making them likely to get stuck at local optima 
			People might settle for bad solutions because they don't know better or have difficulty in reaching these solutions (because of coordination problems or ability of powerful actors to veto possible changes)
Lu Hong and Scott Page (2004) - use mathematical models to argue that diversity of viewpoints help groups find better solutions - different individuals when confronting a problem see different landscapes - they organize the set of possible solutions in different ways 
	At a group level, Page and Hong find that "diversity trumps ability" - groups with high diversity of internal viewpoints are better able to identify optima than groups composed of much smarter individuals with more homogenous viewpoints
	By putting together their diverse views, the former are able to map out more of the landscape and identify possible solutions that would be invisible to groups of individuals with more similar perspectives
		However the social process through which individuals bring together diverse points of view is not modeled
		Their argument suggests that actors' different points of view need to be exposed directly to each other to identify benefits and drawbacks of different poitns of view 
		Also even if groups are able to identify good solutions they may not be able to reach them
			Actors who benefit from the status quo might be able to use political and social power to block movement towards peaks and compel movement towards solutions that have lower social and greater individual benefit 
			Individuals might not be able to agree on ranking - they might have diverse interests as well as diverse understandings of the world - they might also differ on their ability to ensure their interests are prosecuted
				Recent work in political theory (Knight 1992, Johnson and Knight 2011), economics (Bowles and Naidu, 2008), political science (Hacker and Pierson 2010) and sociology details how powerful actors may be able to compel weaker ones to accept solutions that are to the advantage of the former, but that have lower overall social benefits.
			Relative equality of power works - individuals in settings with relatively equal power relations are more likely to converge on solutions with broad social benefits  - these equal power relations not only make convergence easier but might also stimulate the process of search for such solutions
			Participating in the search for solutions and in decision making demands resources and if these resources are concentrated in a small set of actors with similar interests and perspectives - the solutions they will find will be fewer and worse than if a variety of actors can also search

Markets and Hierarchies as Ways to Solve COmplex Problems
	* Markets or hierarchies work better in solving complex problems than democracy?
		* Market advocates build on the work of Hayek - market based forms of organization do better in eliciting information and putting it to good work than collective organization
	* Advocates of hierarchy include Richard Thaler/Cass Sunstein - advocating Libertarian Paternalism - combination of top down mechanism design and institutions designed to guide choices vs constrain them
* Hayek's account of the informational benefits of markets is groundbreaking - first effort to analyze social institutions as information processors
	* Much of human knowledge is tacit/practical but crucial - we need to gather this dispersed fragmentary knowledge
	* Since no one person can know all that is required to allocate resources properly, there is a SOCIAL mechanism for information processing
	* Three possible mechanisms identified: Central planning, planning by monopolistic industries, decentralized planning by individuals 
		* centralized/semi-centralized planning are especially poor at dealing with constant flows of major/minor changes through which an economy approaches balance
		* The virtue of the price system is to compress diffuse, tacit knowledge about specific changes in circumstances into a single index - guide individuals as to how they ought to respond to changes elsewhere 
			* The insight that information is conveyed through prices re whether we need to buy whatever input is crucial and beauty, yet has limits
			* It suggests markets will be best at conveying a particular kind of information about some underlying fact - relative scarcity of different goods
			* Market signals about relative scarcity are always distorted - prices embed information about many other economically important factors
			* It also doesn't really help in solving more complicated problems - might also be about discovering new goods or new forms of allocations 
			* Hayeks' detestation for projects with collective goals led him to discount the ways in which aggregate knowledge might work to solve collective vs individual problems
				* Foreclosed forms of exchange - interaction of different individuals and exchange of viewpoints
				* There is rich literature on the global consequences of market society - do markets reduce power inequalities or reinforce them in modern democracies?
				* Markets allow individuals to break away from harmful relationships but also provides greater voice to those with mroe money - facilitating and fostering asymmetries of wealth which might be directly/indirectly transferred to asymmetries of political influence - untrammeled markets are associated with gross income inequalities?
	* Hierarchy - thaler and susntein suggests it does better
		* Choice architects (people with responsibility for organizing the context in which people make decisions) can design institutions to spur people to take better vs worse choices?
		* Politics of nudging, plea for benefits of hierarchy in organizations/government - the superiors are empowered to create broad schemes that will shape choices of many individuals - it flows from positions or authority within government 
		* The authors don't have normative objections to coercion provided it is proportionate, directed towards legitimate ends, constrained by democratic controls - worrying about hierarchy Thaler and Sunstein assume inhibit the unconstrained exchange fo views which are essential to problem solving
			* Bureaucracies are a political achievement but problematic - poor at transmitting useful information up the chain - they encourage a culture of conformity in order to increase predictability and static efficiency (noted by Max Weber)
			* The authors ignores the problem of feedback - how do the architects figure out better choices?
				* Some methods are suggested like introspection, experimentatl techniques, results from social sciences?
				* Systematically ignoring feedback from consumers - citizens as passive consuemrs who are not active participants in democratic decision making
				* Not taking advantage of diversity
			* Architects similar to each other in background and training will as a group see a much more limited set of possibilities than a group of randomly selected members of the population 
			* Even if they wanted feedback it would be hard to elicit it - inequality of power dampens real exchange of viewpoints 
			* Libertarian parternalism is flawed not just because of choice restrictions but because it makes assumptiosn about choice architect's ability to figure out default choices and blocks their channels for learning better

Demcoracy as a way to solve complex problems?
	Democracy has its problems - partisanship and political rivalry, rancor - early 20th century progressives and heirs deplore partisanship and political rivalry, preferring technocracy 
	A commitment to democratic improvements is a commitment to making power relations more equal - it is also well suited to exposing points of view to each other that lead to identifying better solutions - invovles debates
	The proposals to replace traditional democratic forms with those that involve deliberation where people will strive to be open minded and transcend their interests is utopian??
	Politics is about the problem of diversity - partial and self interested debate can have epistemological benefits
	Approaches based on diversity provide a better foundation for thinking about the epistemic benefits of democracy than arguments of Condorcet
		The argument suggests democracy can foster communication among individuals with highly diverse viewpoints - cognitive democracy - democratic arrangements that take best advantage of the cognitive diversity of their population 
			Unequal societies select a smaller range of viewpoints - those of powerful people
		Micro-level work which speak to this issue
			Hugo Mercier and Dan Sperber - argumentative account of reasoning - where reasoning is not meant to reach right answers but to evaluate the weaknesses of others' arguments and come up with good arguments to support one's position - confirmation bias and motivated reasoning are rife - the quality of arguments is significantly better when actors engage in real debates - groups are typically better at solving problems than the best individual in the group
		A separate line of research in experimental social psychology - indicates that problem solving groups produce more solutions, which outsiders assess as better and more innovative, when they contain persistent dissenting minorities and are encouraged to engage in rather than refrain from mutual criticism (Nemeth et al. (2004), Nemeth and Ormiston (2007), and Nemeth (2012))
			Micro-level evidence that political argument will improve problem solving even if we are skeptical about human beings' ability to abstract away from their specific circumstances and interests
				Neither a commitment to deliberation or standard rationality is required for argument to help solve problems 
				Even the most homogenous seeming societies contain great diversity of opinion and interest - sometimes political actors have to build coalitions with others holding dissimilar views and have to content with others holding opposed perspectives in order to persuade uncommitted others to favour their interpretation rather than another 
		* Confirmation bias that political advocates have are subject to crucial benefits as long as it is tempered by the ability to evaluate good arguments in context 
		* Lazer and Friedman (2007) find on the basis of simulations that problem solvers connected via linear networks (in which there are few links) will find better solutions over the long run than problem solvers connected via totally connected networks (where all the nodes are linked to each other). In a totally connected network, actors copy the best immediately visible solution quickly, driving diversity from the system, while in a linear network, different groups explore the space around different solutions for a much longer period.
