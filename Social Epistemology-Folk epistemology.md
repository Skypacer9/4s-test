1. the most influential tradition in Western epistemology has focused on how individual epistemic agents using their own cognitive faculties can pursue the truth - the most promising way to do so is by use of one's reasonoing as applied to clear and distinct ideas
2. Even early empiricists insisted that knowledge be acquired through intellectual self-reliance
3. Social epistemology proceeds on the idea that we often rely on others in our pursuit of truth - social epistemology's core questions revolve around the nature, scope, epistemic significance of this reliance

Why has social epistemology only come into its own in the last few decades - individualistic self understanding of early modern science, the centrality philosophers have traditionally ascribed to the problem of skepticism - the pursuit of truth as a solitary endeavor - epistemology itself centers on the challenges and practice of individual agents

There was a movement analogous to social epistemology developed in the middle of the 20th century - sociological in nature, focused on how science is actually practiced - aiming to debunk idealized accounts of science in orthodox epistemology and mid century philosophy of science - STrong program in the sociology of knowlege like Harry Collins and David Bloor - providing a philosophical rationale for challenging the notion of objective truth - arguing that facts are not discovered by science but constructed, constituted - Bloor advocated for the symmetry thesis according to which scientists' beliefs are to be explained by social factors - regardless of whether these beliefs are true or false, rational or irrational -> denying the explanatory relevance of such things as facts and evidence
6. The interdisciplinary field of STS which emerged in the 1960s and 70s and borrowed from sociology, policy studies, HPS and parts of history, political science, and athropology, theorists in this field urged that we see science and technology tehmselves as deeply embedded in social practices
7. Michel Focault developed a radically political view of knowledge and science, arguing practices of knowledge seeking are driven by quests f or power and social domination

3.1. Testimony
* Testimony is paradigmatic - the act in which one agent reports something to an audience - an audience who accepts the report on the speaker's authority acquires a testimony based belief -> a basic source of justificiation - a perceptual belief can be justified even without your having reasons to assume that perception is reliable 
* Reductionism - those who deny that testimony is a basic source of justification hold that testimony based beliefs are justified only if the audience has adequate independent reasons to regard the speaker's testimony as trustworthy -> reductionism - proposes that the justification of these beliefs can be reduced to justifications provided by other sources - honesty in speakers and credulity in audiences are as much a part of our natural psychological endowment, as worthy of being relied upon in belief formation, as is the faculty of perception
