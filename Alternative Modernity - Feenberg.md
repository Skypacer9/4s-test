Underdetermination and Public Intervention:
Feenberg's recent book Critical Theory of TEchnology (1991) tried to establish three main points:
1) Technological design is socially relative, contrary to deterministic arguments or theories of technical neutrality
2) The unequal distribution of social influence over technological design contributes to social injustice
3) There are at least some instances in which public involvement in the design of devices and systems have had significant results (chapters 5 and 7)

The Duhem- Quine principle in the philosophy of science - the inevitable lack of logically compelling reasons for preferring one competing scientific theory to another - ==the thesis holds that rational technical principles are insufficient by themselves to determine design==
Successful design respects technical principles - but there are several possible designs with which to achieve the same or similar objectives - no decisive technical reason to prefer one to the other  - technical choices are thus ==underdetermined== and the final decision between alternatives ultimately depends on the 'fit' between them and the interests and beliefs of the  various social groups that influence the design process

* Technological designs are negotiated achievements involving many partners not rational inspirations that spring full blown from the mind of an individual genius or pure laboratory research - the design process is the place where the various social actors interested in developing technology first gain a hearing - a variety of stakeholders guarantees the design represents many interests - they wield their influence by offering or withholding resources, defining the purposes of the devices they require, fitting them into existing technical means and imposing directions - they are social expressions of these actors 
	* this argument is also central to the recent constructivist sociology of technology
	* Technical code to describe those features of technologies that reflect the hegemonic values and beliefs that prevail in the design process - such codes are usually invisible because like culture, they appear self-evident
		* These can include sizes (for adults), the basic definitions of many technical objects as they have become universally, culturally accepted features of daily life (like the telephone, automobile, refrigerator) - each new instance of standard technologies must conform to its defining 'code' to be recognizable and acceptable to people like us - constructivists sometimes call the establishment of such codes 'black boxing' because one does not question what is 'inside' the technology once its definition is generally accepted
		* Taking Langdon Winner's proposal seriously - that technology is a new kind of legislation shaping our way of life, not that different from law 
			* As technology becomes more central to more and more aspects of lives, its legislative authority increases. But if technology is so powerful, then surely it should be measured by the same democratic standards as other political institutions. By those standards the design process appears outmoded and unfair.
			* Currently owners of corporations, military bureaucrats, and professional organizations of technologists have far more influence over the direction of technology than ordinary citizens - though they might not obstruct technical progress to further their own interests - but they might channel progress in a particular direction compatible with those interests  - under the influence of the market, they represent a wide range of social needs and achieve many social goods - but not the same as democratic control of technology - while markets in many goods are desirable, they lack the public character, the element of debate and conscious coordination associated with democratic action - consumer sovereignty isn't entirely accurate
			* even with the help of state regulation consumers usually cannot break through the imposing facade of fiscal power and the technical resources of modern corporations
			* I==ssues of control over technical decisions rarely surface in the context of the market - those in charge of technical destiny meet few obstacles in reproducing their technical power in relations with consumers - their interest in maintaining that power is a kind of bottom line inscribed in all technical decisions, biasing them in favour of centralization and hiearchy
				* The assembly line exemplifies the biasing of design by powerful interests - the history of deskilling of the labour process under capitalism - these inequalities are by no means transcended in contemporary capitalism despite profound changes in technology and management - a particularly significant example of how technical decisions support inequality and injustice - there are also case studies in medicine and computer design
			* The technical code of medical experimentation defined it in terms of the interests of scientific research and industrial product testing - this offered subjects basic protection from exploitation, but ignored terminally ill patients' demands for experimentatal participation. In the case of French videotex, a computer network installed on the scale of an entire nation through the distribution of mililons of free terminals was intended to give access to information - users' interests in communicating with each otehr was ignored. In each case public intervention (AIDS patients and network users) - significantly altered the system to accomdoate excluded interests. 
			* Technical systems canont be considered finished until they have withstood the social tests that expose them to a wide range of public influences and concerns excluded in the design phase. 
		* Legitimacy and rationality - democratization of technical change requires opening of the design process to actors who lack financial/cultural/political capital  - this might not impeded progress and might avoid later problems, also ensuring adequate representation of undervalued interests which conflict with centralized, elite control of design - the long term implications of more democratic designs are earth shaking given the significant imprint of elite control on many aspects of society
