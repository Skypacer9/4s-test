3. Can distinguish between complicated - complexity refers to something composed of many interconnected parts - understanding a complicated phenomena requires an analytic approach, whereas complex phenomena require a synthetic or systematic approach  - the properties of the parts can be understood only within the context of the larger whole - systems thinking is contextual 
	1. Systems science shows that living systems cannot be understood by analysis - the properties of the parts are not intrinsic properties but can be understood only within the context of the larger whole'

	1. having a complex view doesn't mean having a complete view 

## 2.2 Complex Systems and Complex Adaptive Systems

* Complex is often associated with system - this can generally be defined as a set of elements that stand in interaction - it can be considered an interconnected set of elements coherently organized in a way that achieves something
* A system is a collection of interconnected elements with a purpose - it has its own behaviour, different from parts, evolving over time according to changes that can concern the system and each of its essential components 
* Systems are organic, global, organized entities, made up of many different parts, aimed at performing a certain function - if one removes a part, its nature and function are modified - the parts have a specific architecture and their interaction makes the system behave different from its parts
* Systems evolve over time and most are resilient to change
* Elements are often other systems - these are  based on a systemic hierarchy that allows the control of elements ensuring they act in a coordinated and harmonious manner
* Slaving principle - elements at a lower hierarchical level are slaves to the upper level and overall system
* The interconnections among elements are more important than the elements themselves? high density and a variety of interconnections??
* Complex adaptive systems - an open system consisting of various elements interacting with each other in a linear and nonlinear way 
* All CAS share three characteristics: evolution, aggregate behaviour, anticipation - they can all evolve and learn, adapt the the envrionment and change by processing information and building models capable of assessing whether adaptation is useful 
* Complex adaptive systems present an aggregate behaviour that emerge as a novelty from the interaction of parts e.g.
	* immune system: ability to distinguish self from others
	* economy: overall network of supply and demand?
	* Ecology: taken to be the overall food web or patterns of flow of energy and materials
	* Embryo: overall structure

* CAS can also anticipate changes -> these can cause large changes in aggregate behaviour even if tehse don't true
* Socioeconomic behaviour are CAS - they are multidimensional and evolve by modifying their dimensions and links between them - knowledge of these phenomena must consider their complex nature
* Measurements in the social sciences have typical characteristics that differ from the natural sciences - definition of systems of indicators capable of capturing the different aspects of phenomena analysed

# 3. Measurement in the Social Sciences

* Science is a result of a dialogue between logic and evidence - generated from theoretical-formal level (where theories and hypotheses are developed and abstract concepts with their mutual relations are specified), empirical - where hypotheses are verified through empirical data
* Knowledge develops between the theory and observations
* Different types of data can be generated from the same empirical observations based on different theoretical framework
* The relationship between model and data is the product of measurement
* If empirical observations are consistent with the model, the latter is a good description of reality
* They are falsifiable - there is a context in which a specific model is inconsistent

## 3.1 Meausrement: Definitions and Main Aspects
* A measurement is defined as 'any method' by which a unique and reciprocal correspondence is established between all or some of the magnitudes of a kind and all or some of the numbers, integral, rational, or real - so called representational approach - measurement is the numerical representation of facts regarding the entities measured 
* Measurement is an activity that determines a shift from the plane of reality to the plane of numbers in which we try to encode them
* MEasures have to be based on uniform procedures to collect, score, and report numerical results - standardised - ensuring foreign components reprersenting the error of observation are isolated or minimized 
* Two types of error can be distinguished. Random error refers to all the factors that interfere with the measurement of any phenomenon and are ineradicable in the process - influences the reliability - consistency of a measurement model in terms of the degree of accuracy and precision with which the instrument measures and the ability to produce consistent measurements - the lower the random error, the higher the level of reliability. ==An instrument affected by it may overestimate or underestimate the magnitude of an attribute measured in a certain object== 
	* Two types of systematic errors: methodological errors - error of definition/detection of the attribute to  be observed, specific error introduced by observer in the use of the observation procedure 
* To reduce errors, all measurements must rely on a set of assumptions of different types:
	* theoretical assumptions related to the meanings given to the phenomenon measured
	* procedural assumptions - related to rules of correspondence used in assigning numbers to observations
	* Statistical assumptions - related to the main characteristics of s tatistical methods - used for analysis

## 3.2. Measurement in the Social Sciences: Systems of Indicators and Their Construction

* Lazarsfeld emphasized in the social sciences, measurement has a typical character which makes them not comparable to the natural sciences
* Operationalisation - process through which theory and abstract concepts are translated into measurable variables - operationalised property of an object 
	* measuring phenomena means trying to understand their nature - dealing with measurements means dealing with systems of indicators
	* indicators relates concepts to reality - indicators are purposeful statistics 
	* Given the complex and multidimensional nature of socioeconomic phenomena - their analysis involves the identification of different basic indicators connected in a system 
	* A system of indicators is not a simple collection of measures, but a complex system - indicators within a system are interconnected, and new properties typical of the system emerge from interconnections - they have to follow a set of rules codified in a step by step process, the so called hierarchical design
	* Defining a phenomena is not an easy task - depends on researchers' pov - small window through which they observe reality and make hypotheses on it
		* Identification of latent variables, each of which is an aspect to be observed - reflect the nature of the phenomenon - which is consistent with the conceptual model 
		* Sometimes latent variables have one dimension, sometimes they have two or more dimensions - there isn't necessarily a direct correspondence between one latent variable and one indicator - the multi indicator is prefereable, for each latent variable, several indicators are identified and selected - increases measurement accuracy and precision
* The measurement model describes the relationship between a construct and its indicators - the reflective and formative - in reflective measurement, causality is from the construct to the measures - measures are considered the effects of an underlying latent construct - correlations between idnicators are explained by the measurement model, two uncorrelated indicators cannot measure the smae construct - typical in psychoemtric research
* Formative models - measure socioeconomic phenomena where indicators cause a latent variable - indicators are not replaceable - changing an indicator will change the construct - internal consistency is of minimal importance 
* The choice of the measurement model does not depend on the researcher but on its appropriateness to the phenomenon that one intends to study - if the direction of the relationship is from the construct to the measures, we have a reflective model. If the direction is from the measures to the construct, we have a formative model
* Synthesis can concern different aspects of a multi-indicator system
	* The synthesis of statistical units aims to aggregate the units in order to create macro-units for comparison, with reference to indicators of interest - the statistical methods taht allow for this are part of the cluster analysis
	* The synthesis of statistical indicator aims to aggregate the values referring to several indicators for each unit of observation, obtaining a synthetic measure - from a technical pov, the statistical methods used in this case can belong to two approaches: aggregative-compensative and nonaggregative

## 3.3. Synthesis of Multi-indicators Systems

* The complex and multidimensional nature of socioeconomic phenomena requires the adoption of different measures to to analyse and understand them. The measurement process in the social sciences is associated with the construction of systems of indicators, which makes it possible to measure phenomena that would not otherwise be measurable -> complex adaptive systems like the phenomena they measure. Complex systems require a synthetic approach to understand the phenomena as a whole. The use of basic indicators are criteria for summarising them
	* An indicator is not simply raw statistical information - a measure organically linked to a conceptual model aimed at describing different aspects of reality
	* A constructed variable related to a specific aspect or dimension of a complex phenomenon - they are obtained by synthesizing elementary indicators according to established criteria and rules

## 3.4. The Aggregative Compensative Approach


* Using a mathematical function of basic indicators - a measure based on sub-indicators - there is no shared method of weighting these sub-indicators. 
* Synthesis is not necessarily a number 
* Constructing a single composite index can be problematic - a more appropriate choice would be to use a dashboard - avoiding the arbitrary choice of functional form and weighting scheme, observing a phenomenon from multiple points of view - however this does not allow for a simple and direct understanding of the phenomena under consideration
* Constructing a composite is not an easy task and involves: the selection of basic indicators, whether and how to noramlise them, aggregation procedure
* Most of these choices, despite being guided by knowledge of the phenomenon, are subjective and often considered non-scientific
* Composites are widely disseminated and used in the scientific literature and policymakers
* The construction of composite indicators invovles:
	* 1. Definition of the phenomenon
	* 2. Selection of the basic indicators
	* 3. Exploratory analysis of basic indicators
	* 4. Normalisation of individual indicators
	* 5. Aggregation of normalised indicators
	* 6. Index validation 

The first two steps are hierarchically ordered and theoretical but not considered separate from the statistical-methodological ones (the other three)
	* measurement in the social sciences begins with the definition of the phenomenon - the concept has to be referred to and inserted within a theoretical framework that provides meaning - the choice of the measurement model depends on the appropriateness of the phenomenon to be measured and the nature and direction of relationships between constructs and measures 
	* The reflective measurement model is widely used in the psychological and management sciences
		* One of the main methods -> factor analysis
		* However even if the phenomenon is well defined and the matrix composed of indicators of good quality, it is not necessarily the case that the composite index is valid 
	* The general rule is all dimensions of a phenomenon must be represented and measured using at least one indicator - each latent variable can be defined and measured by using a single indicator - it is weak and assumes the existence of direct correspondence between one latent variable and one indicator - it is preferable to adopt a multi-indicator approach, using several indicators for each dimension 
		* there is also a risk of redundancy - but it can be intentional to increase the probability of complete reception of the message in the presence of noise or disturbances
		* The exclusion of an elementary indicator always affects the latent variable and the composite indicator -> indicators cause the latent variable and remove one change to the latent variable 
	* The exploratory analysis of basic indicators is a methodological step that aims to answer important questions - is the latent structure of the synthetic index well defined, are the chosen indicators sufficient to describe the phenomenon? Application of multivariate statistical techniques to study the latent structure of data and analyse the relatinoships among the indicators within the system 
		* PCA (principal component analysis) - reciprocal relationships between phenomena
		* correlation is a symmetric concept that does not refer to a cause and effect link but the tendency of one variable to change in relation to another 
	* Correlations are not decisive - measurement model depends on the definition of the phenomenon and the consequent nature of a latent variable
	* importance is in the case of a reflexive measurement model 
	* PCA can be used to identify the dimensionality of the phenomenon, define weights, and as an aggregation method
		* weights are chosen such that the principal components are orthogonal - the first component accounts for the maximum possible proportion of the variance of the set of original variables
		* eigenvalues are variances in principal components 
			* if variances explained by the first component is high, most indicators correlate and represent a single aspect of the phenomenon
			* if the variance is not very high - there are several groups of indicators - several latent factors and the necessity of constructing more than one composite
	* Normalisation is required to make indicators comparable because they often present different measurement units and ranges - transforming into pure numbers
		* a delicate step because it can change the distribution and internal variability of the indicators
		* Conducting a robustness analysis 
		* however normalisation does not solve the problem of combining different measures 
		* Necessary to define the polarity of the basic indicators - the sign of the relationship between the indicator itself and the phenomenon
		* Double polarity - indicator presenting a positive polarity below a certain threshold and a negative polarity above i
		* Ranking -> assigning the same rank equal to the mean of ranks they would have had in case of different values  - purifying indicators from measurement unit 
		* The choice of weighting has a large impact on values and consequently on the meaning of composites
		* There are different approaches to the weighting issue, trace to three categories
			* giving to all indicators the same weight
			* weights derived from statistical characteristics of data and attributed as a result of a statistical method, for example PCA
			* weights assigned to individual indicators based on judgments of public/expert
			* ranking assumes the same distance between every unit - the differences between cannot be evaluated because absolute level information is lost
		* Aggregation methods - based on the degree of toleration/substitutability among basic indicators - called substitutable if a deficit in one component can be compensated for by a surplus in another -  the assumption of component implies the adoption of additive aggregation methods (like arithmetic mean) - or nonsubstitable (geomteric mean)
		* Partially compensative method - some can be compensated up to a point - but what's the threshold
		* Benefit of the doubt - based on data envelopment analysis - linear programming technique - measuring the relatively efficiency of decision-making units on the basis of multiple inputs and outputs - the synthetic measure is obtained as the weighted sum of normalised indicators relative to a benchmark
* The construction of a composite involves different subjective choices - selection of individual indicators, choice of aggregation model, weights of indicators
