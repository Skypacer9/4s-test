submitted abstract:

Technocratic governance, driven by optimization metrics and price signals, systematically obscures wicked social and ecological problems. Goodhart's and Campbell's laws reveal a core paradox: once a metric becomes a target, it ceases to be a valid measure. Yet contemporary artificial intelligence development is born from and remains locked into this logic, optimizing for engagement, efficiency, and profit while sidelining adaptive, participatory, and holistic alternatives, threatening to bring about a new level of mass disempowerment if unchecked. Beginning with an epistemic justification for validating harms and testimony even in situations lacking full causal comprehension—resisting the framing of "inevitable tradeoffs" that forecloses alternatives—this paper argues that escaping this trap requires examining and extending upon real-world sociotechnical systems that have experimented with different objective functions. Drawing on critiques of rationalist imposition (Feenberg, Bookchin) and the limits of price-based information (Shalizi), we analyze two alternative pathways: the decentralized web movement, which foregrounds protocol-level participation, a culture of subsidiarity and self-determination, and the social investing sector, which attempts to embed non-price values into capital allocation.

Through these case studies, we assess how each fares in keeping objective functions multiple, revisable, and accountable to the lifeworlds they claim to serve, and aim to identify design principles for alternative AI development. These principles do not propose a utopian blueprint but offer concrete starting points for altering the current trajectory of AI toward more accountable and context-sensitive forms of intelligence. We aim to supplement a traditional academic paper with diagrams, designs, simulations, and interactive roleplay.
