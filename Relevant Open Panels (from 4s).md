
1. Corporate Power: On the ghost-management of knowledge 

On corporations and how they shape knowledge ecologies -> how they shape and redefine public policy, public interest, market economies
In many sectors, dominant corporations have the capacity to influence socio-political structures and markets through orchestrated production and dissemination of research expertise and obstruction against adverse narratives, as well as through different forms of capture over professionals, regulations, technology, civil society, or media. 'Ghost-management' refers to these systematic corporate strategies to shape science, culture, policy agenda and public narratives, to hide risks and downplay harms of specific products and to influence experts, opinion leaders and policymakers, while keeping corporations' roles obscured or in the shadows. Ghost-management encompasses practices such as: planning, funding, scripting of research, authorship and sponsorship arrangements, control voer data, study design and selective publication, deployment of key opnion leaders and apparently independent intermediaries, revolving doors, litigation and lobbying strategies, the architecture of social programs and public expenditures.
	This can influence or manipulate agenda-setting in public policy by steering what counts as evidence, which problems are seen as urgent, whose risks matter, what regulatory solutions are feasible.

3. Model welfare: quantification and politics of the future in Africa
(data, quantification and scientometrics, decolonial and postcolonial STS)
Welfare systems rely on extensive knowledge about the population to plan, finance, expend limited resources to the benefit of those most in need - with notable exceptions, their often fragmented national identification and statistical systems represent significant challenges for Africa's emerging welfare states' capacity to establish categories of entitlement, target groups for intervention, and - in the current moment of the demographic transition - model pathways towards positive and inclusive welfare future
How are imaginaries of future welfare shaped by globally circulating model systems and their associated standards of measurement? How do these models shape the ways in which welfare data are collected, adapted, and used in specific settings? How is the epistemic authority of the model distributed across international organizations, national governments, and users of the model? Which futures are made visible, while obscuring other paths for intervention? And how is the future enacted as a site of welfare politics?
Papers may critically engage the genealogies, infrastructures, practices of datafying welfare and/or its projected futures in different African welfare settings - including social protection, health care, education, disaster risk reduction, among other areas - to explore the making of techno-scientific welfare futrue sin sites of cirtical and intersecting trasnformations - including shifts in demographic composition, innovations in public sector digitalization, increasing calls for reclaiming social rights and citizenship in view of big tech's creep into social domains. Contributions may focus on the work and practices of national statistical organizations, public-private collaborations, citizen science, more than human participations in the production of models of future welfare.

5. The Politics of Data and Data Infrastructures (Data, Quantification (and Scientometrics), Infrastructure, Information, Computing and Media Technology)
THe use and exchange values of data have skyrocked. Data collected range from consciously produced and shared data via platforms such as social media, tracking apps and search engines to involuntarily collected or structurally extracted data, often with little to no consent, such as meter data, road condition and traffic data, transaction data, and health data. This open panel focuses on the politics of these data and associated data infrastructures (data centres, broadband networks, related pipelines) - the politics of data centres on the social dynamics driven by its paradoxical nature: data is dually necessary for the provision of essential public and private services, the organization of economic and political life, and contemporary forms of governance while reproducing and exacerbating inequality, climate change, and security risks
Incongruous nature of data and data infrastructures by addressing the following questions: What are the roles of communities and individuals in producing responsible data for the digital transition? What theories and methods are useful in understanding the politics of data and data infrastructures? How are data used, stored, and managed and how do these practices affect communities and inidivduals.

12. How Critique Stabilizes Technoscientific Power
Disciplines and the Social Organization of Science and Technology; Information, Computing and Media Technology; Economics, Markets, Value/Valuation
For decades, STS scholarship has analyzed technoscientific systems: exposing contradictions, destabilizing conceptual foundations, tracing entanglements with power. Yet technoscientific elites often consolidate control, and systems frequently accelerate despite incisive intervention. The panel poses a basic question: How and why has STS work increasingly coincided with teh stabilization and intensification of technoscientific power? Absortpion mechanisms enable systems to metabolize critique as evidence of institutional reflexivity, reinforcing legitimacy, consolidating power, destabilizing counternarratives. -Papers may examine how systems perform responsiveness while maintaining operational closure; why ""responsible,"" ""ethical,"" or participatory reforms often stabilize dominant arrangements; and how to distinguish transformation (structural correction) from reflexive performance (power consolidation). Counterexamples of effective interventions are also welcome.
